Abstract

This dissertation presents the design and development of a Flutter-based analytical application for selected microwave passive components. The main aim of the project was to provide an accessible and interactive software platform that supports the study of microwave network behavior through theory-based calculation and visualization. The application focuses on four representative components: the Resistive Divider, Wilkinson Divider, Rat-Race Coupler, and Branch-Line Hybrid.
The project began with a review of the theoretical principles of microwave passive devices and their corresponding analytical models. Based on these models, a modular software architecture was developed using Flutter and Dart to enable cross-platform deployment and a consistent user interface. Each module allows users to enter key parameters and obtain analytical outputs through an interactive interface. The calculation process includes detailed step-by-step derivations showing how frequency-dependent complex S-parameters are obtained and then converted to magnitude in dB form using the standard 20ˇÁlog?? transformation. In addition to numerical calculation, graphical rendering methods were implemented to display component schematics, frequency-dependent S-parameter plots, and animated signal behaviour.
The developed system was evaluated through software-level functional testing and analytical verification against expected theoretical results. The results show that the application performs reliably within the scope of ideal microwave analysis. In particular, the Wilkinson Divider and Rat-Race Coupler modules demonstrated outputs consistent with theoretical expectations under different input conditions, while the Resistive Divider and Branch-Line Hybrid modules provided useful reference demonstrations of standard behavior. The interface was designed with consistency across all modules, placing S-parameter plots directly below the parameter input section for easier comparison and analysis.
Overall, the project demonstrates that modern cross-platform development frameworks can be effectively applied to microwave engineering education and theory-oriented analysis. Although the current system is limited to idealized analytical models and does not replace full-wave simulation or experimental measurement, it provides a practical foundation for future extension as an educational and analytical tool.
Keywords: microwave passive components, Flutter, analytical application, Wilkinson divider, Rat-Race coupler, Branch-Line hybrid, educational software

Chapter 1 Introduction

1.1 Research Background

Microwave passive components play an essential role in modern wireless communication systems. Among these components, power dividers and directional couplers are widely used in signal distribution, power combining, impedance matching, antenna feeding networks, balanced circuits, and measurement systems. Their performance directly affects signal transmission quality and the operation of RF and microwave systems. For this reason, a solid understanding of coupler and divider operation is important for students and engineers in the field of wireless communication.
In RF circuit education, the analysis of couplers and dividers normally involves transmission-line theory, scattering parameters, impedance transformation, and even- odd mode analysis. Although these topics are fundamental, they are often mathematically intensive and may be difficult for students to visualize using only lecture notes or static textbook examples. In many cases, learners can follow the derivation process on paper but still face difficulty connecting the equations to actual circuit behavior and signal flow.
At the same time, software tools have become increasingly important in engineering learning and design. Numerical tools such as MATLAB and specialized RF software can assist with calculation and verification, but they are not always optimized for

lightweight, interactive, step-by-step educational use on smart devices. Therefore, a mobile or cross-platform application that combines formulas, derivation steps, visualization, and numerical results may offer a more direct and convenient learning approach.
Flutter is a modern cross-platform development framework that enables the creation of interactive applications with a consistent user interface across different devices. Its widget-based architecture and graphical capabilities make it suitable for educational engineering applications. In this project, Flutter was selected as the development platform to implement an application for the analysis of four representative microwave components: the Resistive Divider, Wilkinson Divider, Rat-Race Coupler, and Branch- Line Hybrid. The calculation procedures and design formulas used in the application are mainly based on the EE6128 RF Circuits in Wireless Communication course, to present the working principles of these components in a clearer and more interactive way.
1.2 Problem Statement

The study of microwave couplers and dividers often relies on manual derivation and static theoretical explanation. While this approach is academically important, it may not always provide an intuitive understanding of how power is divided, how ports are isolated, or how phase relationships affect output signals. Many students encounter difficulty when attempting to relate theoretical formulas to circuit structure, signal propagation, and final numerical results.

Existing software tools can perform RF-related calculations, but they may present several limitations in an educational context. Some tools focus mainly on professional design rather than conceptual explanation, while others do not provide a clear step-by- step derivation process for beginners. In addition, many solutions are desktop-oriented and are not designed as portable learning tools for quick use on smart devices.
Another challenge is that different microwave components require different analytical methods. For example, a resistive divider can be described using basic circuit and power relations, whereas a Wilkinson divider involves quarter-wave transformers and isolation resistance, and couplers such as the Rat-Race and Branch-Line Hybrid require even-odd mode analysis and scattering-matrix interpretation. Without a unified platform, students must switch between lecture notes, hand calculations, and multiple software environments, which reduces learning efficiency.
Therefore, there is a need for an interactive application that integrates theoretical derivation, user-controlled parameter input, graphical visualization, and result calculation within a single platform. Such an application should be portable, easy to use, and suitable for both learning and quick analytical reference. This is the main motivation for the present project.
1.3 Research Aim and Objectives

The main objective of this project is to develop a Flutter-based application for coupler and divider analysis on smart devices.
The specific objectives are as follows:

1. To design and implement a cross-platform application using Flutter for the analysis of selected microwave couplers and dividers.
2. To include four representative components in the application, namely the Resistive Divider, Wilkinson Divider, Rat-Race Coupler, and Branch-Line Hybrid.
3. To present the analytical procedures of these components in a step-by-step format based on the formulas and concepts taught in EE6128 RF Circuits in Wireless Communication.
4. To provide interactive input settings and graphical visualization in order to improve user understanding of signal flow, power division, coupling behavior, and port relationships.
5. To compute and display relevant results such as transmission coefficients, scattering parameters, isolation characteristics, and power distribution.
6. To verify the correctness of the implemented calculations through comparison with theoretical expectations and reference software tools such as MATLAB or other relevant software, where applicable.
1.4 Scope of the Project

This project focuses on the development of a Flutter-based analytical application for four microwave passive components: the Resistive Divider, Wilkinson Divider, Rat- Race Coupler, and Branch-Line Hybrid. The work emphasizes formula-based calculation, structured derivation, and educational visualization rather than full-wave

electromagnetic simulation.

The application allows users to enter selected parameters and observe the corresponding analytical results. In the Resistive Divider module, the app demonstrates matching behavior, transmission coefficients, scattering matrix formulation, and power loss characteristics. In the Wilkinson Divider module, the app supports both equal and unequal power division by allowing the user to vary frequency and split ratio. In the Rat-Race Coupler module, the app includes multi-port excitation control, where users can specify amplitude and phase at different ports and obtain the output response based on the scattering-matrix relation, ?? = ????. In the Branch-Line Hybrid module, the app focuses on the standard single-input case and illustrates the ideal center-frequency behavior as well as the response variation with frequency.
The theoretical formulas implemented in the application are mainly derived from EE6128 course material. The project also includes graphical visualization of circuit structures and animated wave behavior to support conceptual understanding. The developed application is intended for educational and analytical purposes on smart devices.
The scope of this study does not include hardware fabrication, experimental measurement, or full-wave EM modeling using commercial solvers such as CST, HFSS, or ADS. In addition, although the Branch-Line Hybrid is also suitable for generalized multi-port excitation analysis, that extension is not implemented in the current version and is left for future work.

1.5 Significance of the Study

This study is significant from both educational and technical perspectives.

From an educational perspective, the proposed application provides a more interactive way to study microwave passive components. Instead of relying only on static derivations, users can adjust parameters, observe signal behavior, and follow the calculation process step by step. This can help bridge the gap between mathematical theory and physical interpretation, especially for students learning RF circuit analysis for the first time. The application may also serve as a supplementary learning tool for courses related to microwave engineering and wireless communication, particularly EE6128 RF Circuits in Wireless Communication.
From a technical perspective, the project demonstrates how Flutter can be used beyond conventional mobile app development to create engineering-oriented calculation tools. The integration of user input, analytical formulas, dynamic visualization, and modular design shows that cross-platform frameworks can support scientific and educational applications effectively. The project therefore provides a practical example of how software can support RF learning and lightweight analytical analysis.
In addition, the inclusion of four different components in one platform increases the usefulness of the application. Each module highlights different RF concepts, such as resistive loss, impedance transformation, isolation, quadrature phase relationship, and multi-port scattering behavior. As a result, the application is not only a software project, but also a useful learning tool for understanding key coupler and divider principles.

Chapter 2  Literature Review

2.1 Introduction

This chapter reviews the literature related to microwave passive components and interactive engineering applications that are relevant to the present project. Since the proposed system focuses on RF couplers and dividers, this review first discusses the role of passive microwave components in RF systems. It then examines the theoretical and practical background of power dividers, directional couplers, and scattering parameters. In addition, existing software tools and Flutter-based application development are reviewed to support the design choice of the proposed system.
The purpose of this chapter is threefold. First, it provides the theoretical background of the selected components, namely the Resistive Divider, Wilkinson Divider, Rat- Race Coupler, and Branch-Line Hybrid. Second, it summarizes previous studies and existing tools related to microwave analysis and learning. Third, it identifies the research gap that motivates the development of a lightweight and interactive Flutter- based analytical application.
2.2 Microwave Passive Components in RF Systems

Microwave passive components are fundamental elements in RF and wireless communication systems. Unlike active devices, passive components do not provide gain. However, they are essential for signal routing, impedance matching, filtering, coupling, and power distribution. Typical examples include filters, resonators,

matching networks, power dividers, and directional couplers [1], [2].

Among these components, power dividers and couplers are widely used in antenna feed networks, balanced amplifiers, mixers, phased-array systems, and measurement platforms. Their electrical behavior directly affects transmission efficiency, return loss, isolation, and phase response. Therefore, they are important not only in practical engineering design but also in microwave engineering education [3], [4].
Another reason for their importance is that these circuits provide clear examples of how microwave theory is applied in practice. Through the study of dividers and couplers, learners can understand transmission-line behavior, impedance transformation, and multi-port network analysis in a more concrete way. For this reason, these components are commonly used in textbooks and laboratory teaching as representative microwave structures.
2.3 Power Dividers

Power dividers are passive networks used to divide an input signal into two or more output signals. Depending on the circuit structure, a power divider may provide equal or unequal power division, and its performance may vary in terms of loss, matching, and isolation. In the present project, two representative divider types are considered: the Resistive Divider and the Wilkinson Divider.
2.3.1 Resistive Power Divider

The resistive power divider is one of the simplest circuits for signal splitting. Its operation is based on a resistor network, and its topology is relatively easy to analyze.

Due to its simple structure, it is often used as an introductory example in RF circuit study [5].
One major advantage of the resistive divider is its straightforward implementation. Since it does not rely on quarter-wave transmission lines, it can show relatively broadband behavior in some applications. This makes it useful for basic signal distribution and conceptual explanation. Nevertheless, the main drawback of this structure is its inherent loss. Part of the input power is dissipated in the resistors rather than delivered to the output ports. As a result, even when matching conditions are satisfied, the circuit efficiency is lower than that of lossless transmission-line-based dividers [6].
Because of this tradeoff, the resistive divider is usually not regarded as the best choice for high-efficiency microwave power distribution. Even so, it remains valuable in education because it allows students to understand the basic ideas of matching, insertion loss, and power division without the additional complexity of transmission- line transformation. For this reason, it is included in the present application as a foundation for later comparison with more advanced divider structures.
2.3.2 Wilkinson Power Divider

The Wilkinson Power Divider is one of the most important and widely studied microwave dividers. It was originally proposed to achieve equal power division while maintaining matching at all ports and isolation between the output ports [7]. In its conventional two-way form, the structure consists of two quarter-wave transmission-

line branches and one resistor connecting the output ports [8], [9].

The main advantage of the Wilkinson divider is that it can provide low-loss power division under matched conditions. Unlike the resistive divider, it does not dissipate power in the resistor during ideal equal-split operation. Instead, the resistor mainly contributes to output-port isolation when imbalance occurs. This feature gives the Wilkinson divider a strong practical advantage in many RF and microwave systems [10].
Because of its excellent properties, the Wilkinson divider has been widely discussed in textbooks and design studies. Researchers have also extended the original structure to unequal power division, compact layouts, and broadband operation [11], [12]. These developments show that the Wilkinson divider is not only a classical circuit, but also a continuing topic in microwave design research [13].
From the educational perspective, the Wilkinson divider is highly suitable for analytical study because it combines several important concepts, including quarter- wave impedance transformation, matching, isolation, and power distribution. Therefore, it is selected as one of the core modules in the proposed Flutter-based application.
2.4 Directional Couplers

Directional couplers are four-port microwave networks that divide an input signal into different output paths with specific amplitude and phase relations. They are widely used in signal sampling, power combining, balanced circuit design, and measurement

systems. Important performance factors include coupling level, insertion loss, isolation, return loss, and phase difference between output signals.
2.4.1 Rat-Race Coupler

The Rat-Race Coupler, also known as the hybrid ring coupler, is a four-port microwave circuit with a ring-shaped transmission-line structure. It is commonly used in balanced mixers, modulators, phase-comparison systems, and power-combining circuits [14], [15].
One important characteristic of the rat-race coupler is that it behaves as a 180? hybrid. Depending on the excited port and the phase relationship of the input signals, the output power can be directed to different ports while one port remains ideally isolated. This property makes the rat-race coupler highly useful in circuits where phase- sensitive signal distribution is required [16].
The rat-race coupler is commonly analyzed using symmetry, even-odd mode methods, and ideal scattering matrices. These methods make the circuit suitable for both analytical study and software-based implementation. In practical research, many works have focused on compact forms, modified structures, and broadband improvement of the rat-race coupler [17], [18].
In the present project, the Rat-Race Coupler module is particularly important because it supports multi-port excitation analysis. Instead of limiting the user to a single-input case, the module allows users to specify amplitudes and phases at different ports, which can better demonstrate the physical meaning of the scattering-matrix relation.

2.4.2 Branch-Line Hybrid Coupler

The Branch-Line Hybrid, also called the quadrature hybrid coupler, is another important four-port microwave component. It is typically constructed using four quarter-wave transmission-line sections arranged in a rectangular form. At the center frequency, it can ideally provide equal power division, good matching, output-port isolation, and a 90? phase difference between the two output ports [19], [20].
Because of these features, the Branch-Line Hybrid is widely used in balanced amplifiers, mixers, phase shifters, and antenna feeding systems. It is also a standard example in microwave engineering courses because its operation can be explained clearly through even-odd mode analysis and network superposition [21].
Despite its usefulness, the conventional branch-line structure has some limitations. Its performance is frequency-dependent, and the quarter-wave branches may occupy a relatively large physical area. Therefore, many researchers have studied compact, wideband, and modified branch-line couplers for modern microwave applications [22], [23].
In the current project, the Branch-Line Hybrid module focuses on the standard single- input analytical case. This is sufficient for the educational purpose of demonstrating the ideal center-frequency behavior and the change in output response with frequency. A more generalized multi-input extension can be considered in future work.

2.6 Existing Software and Educational Tools

Many software tools are available for RF and microwave analysis. Professional simulation environments can provide accurate numerical calculation, parameter optimization, and electromagnetic verification. Such tools are valuable in engineering design because they support circuit modeling and practical implementation [29], [30]. However, these platforms are mainly designed for professional use. In many cases, they focus more on simulation results than on theoretical explanation. Although they are powerful, they may not always be convenient for beginners who need to understand the derivation process behind the formulas. In addition, some tools are desktop- oriented and are less suitable for quick and portable use on smart devices [31].
In engineering education, interactive learning tools and virtual laboratories have been increasingly discussed as useful supplements to textbooks and lecture materials. These tools can improve understanding by allowing users to change parameters and observe the response immediately. This is especially helpful for microwave topics, where abstract concepts such as coupling, matching, and isolation may be difficult to visualize through static notes alone [32].
Based on this observation, there is still a need for an educationally oriented application that is lightweight, portable, and able to combine formulas, derivation steps, and graphical interaction in one platform. This need provides part of the motivation for the present project.

2.7 Flutter for Engineering and Educational Application

Flutter is a modern cross-platform framework for application development. It allows developers to build mobile, desktop, and web applications from a single code base. Its widget-based structure supports flexible interface design, dynamic state updates, and custom graphical presentation, which are useful in interactive applications [33].
Recent studies have shown that Flutter has been increasingly used in educational systems and application-oriented development because it offers efficient interface construction and a relatively consistent user experience across different devices [34]. Although many of these works are not specifically focused on microwave engineering, the same technical advantages are highly relevant to engineering tools.
For an analytical engineering application, several functions are important: user-defined parameter input, real-time output update, modular page design, and intuitive visual presentation. Flutter is suitable for these requirements because it supports responsive UI behavior and structured software organization [35]. Therefore, it is a reasonable platform for building an educational app for coupler and divider analysis.
2.8 Research Gap

Based on the literature reviewed above, several points can be identified. First, the theory of microwave dividers and couplers is already well established, and many classical as well as improved structures have been reported. Second, scattering- parameter analysis provides a clear and standard framework for multi-port microwave circuits. Third, professional software tools exist, but they are not always designed for

lightweight, derivation-centered learning. Fourth, although Flutter has been applied in educational software development, its use in RF and microwave analytical learning tools remains limited [36].
Therefore, the research gap does not lie in the absence of microwave theory itself. Instead, the gap lies in the limited availability of an integrated application that combines the following features in a single platform: step-by-step derivation, user- controlled parameter input, analytical calculation, schematic visualization, and portable cross-platform implementation [37].
The present project addresses this gap by developing a Flutter-based application for four representative microwave passive components: the Resistive Divider, Wilkinson Divider, Rat-Race Coupler, and Branch-Line Hybrid. In particular, the inclusion of multi-port excitation in the Rat-Race Coupler module extends the learning process beyond the basic single-input case and improves the educational value of the system.

The Resistive Divider, Wilkinson Divider, Rat-Race Coupler, and Branch-Line Hybrid were discussed as representative circuits in RF and microwave engineering. In addition, existing software tools and Flutter-based application development were reviewed from the perspective of interactive learning and lightweight system implementation.
From the reviewed literature, it can be concluded that although the theoretical

principles of couplers and dividers are already mature, there is still a practical need for an application that presents these principles in a more direct, portable, and interactive way. This finding provides the foundation for the system design and methodology described in the next chapter.

3.2.3 Even-Odd Mode Analysis

Even-odd mode analysis is a useful method for analyzing symmetric microwave circuits. It is commonly applied to couplers and hybrid structures because symmetry allows the network to be decomposed into simpler subproblems.

In the even mode, symmetric ports are excited with equal amplitude and equal phase. In the odd mode, the same ports are excited with equal amplitude but opposite phase. Under these two excitation conditions, the symmetry plane behaves differently, which simplifies the analysis of the network.
The total response of the original circuit can then be obtained by superposition of the even-mode and odd-mode solutions. This method is particularly useful for the Rat- Race Coupler and the Branch-Line Hybrid, where port isolation and output phase difference are closely related to circuit symmetry [19], [21], [25].
3.3 Resistive Divider Theory

The Resistive Divider is one of the simplest circuits for RF power division. Unlike transmission-line-based dividers, it uses resistors to distribute the input signal into two output paths. The main advantage of the Resistive Divider is its straightforward implementation. It does not require quarter-wave sections, and its topology can be understood using basic circuit theory. This interface establishes the overall layout and interaction style of the system. The main application interface is shown in Figure 4.2.

Each module page contains the key elements required for interactive analysis, including a parameter input section, a frequency-dependent S-parameter plot positioned directly below the input area, and a step-by-step calculation section. This organization allows the user to move naturally from defining analytical conditions to interpreting the corresponding outputs.
One important design feature is the consistent placement of the S-parameter plots across all modules. In the current implementation, the plot appears immediately below the parameter input section in all four modules: Wilkinson Divider, Branch-Line Hybrid, Resistive Divider, and Rat-Race Coupler. This uniform layout makes it easier for users to observe how changes in input parameters affect the frequency response, and it also reduces the learning effort required when moving from one module to another.
The step-by-step calculation sections are designed to show the complete analytical process in a transparent way. For each module, the calculation is presented in two main stages. Second, the magnitude of each S-parameter is converted to decibel form using the standard transformation 20ˇÁlog??(|S|). This two-stage presentation helps users understand how theoretical formulas lead to the final numerical results commonly used in RF engineering.
The realized interface demonstrates the practical suitability of Flutter for this type of engineering-oriented educational application. Although the specific analytical content differs across modules, the interface remains visually consistent and easy to follow. This is especially important in a learning-based tool, since it allows users to compare different microwave structures within a common software environment.
5.4 Graphical Rendering and Visualization Methods

The graphical schematics in the developed application were not inserted as static images. Instead, they were rendered programmatically in Flutter so that the displayed structures, wave motion, and port states could remain synchronized with the analytical results of each module. This approach was important because the application was intended not only to present numerical outputs, but also to provide an intuitive visual interpretation of microwave signal flow.
In implementation terms, the rendering process combines Flutter widget-based state control with custom drawing. The schematic display of each module was built using CustomPaint and CustomPainter, while dynamic motion was driven by an AnimationController embedded in a StatefulWidget. In several modules, including the Resistive Divider, Rat-Race Coupler, and Branch-Line Hybrid, the repainting process was managed through AnimatedBuilder, allowing the displayed waveforms to update continuously during animation and also to respond to changes in controller values. This design enabled the application to redraw the microwave structures in real time without relying on pre-generated graphical assets.
For the Wilkinson Divider module, the characteristic divider geometry was generated directly from drawing paths. The input transmission line was represented by a straight path, while the two output branches were formed using Path together with quadraticBezierTo(...), producing the curved upper and lower arms of the divider. The branch widths were also adjusted according to calculated impedance-related parameters, so that the visual structure reflected the analytical state of the model. The isolation resistor and output loads were drawn separately using line segments and zigzag resistor symbols, and the travelling waves were generated by tracing sinusoidal offsets along the corresponding paths.
For the Rat-Race Coupler module, the hybrid ring was rendered geometrically using drawCircle(...), while the four ports were positioned by calculating points around the ring from predefined angular locations. Straight connection lines were then drawn outward from the ring to form the port arms. The travelling-wave display was produced programmatically by sampling a sinusoidal function along the propagation direction between two points. 5.5.1 Resistive Divider Module

The resistive power divider module implements a three-port network with equal power division and inherent isolation loss. The implementation consists of several key components organized in the lib/resistive_divider/ directory.
The parameter input interface (rd_input.dart) provides controls for adjusting the operating frequency and characteristic impedance Z?. The frequency range spans from 0.1 GHz to 30 GHz, accommodating both microwave and millimeter-wave applications. Users can also select which port receives the input signal, with the system automatically calculating the resulting power distribution and reflection characteristics.
The calculation steps component (rd_steps.dart) presents the mathematical derivation in four distinct stages. Step 1 establishes the circuit topology and resistance values based on the impedance matching requirements. Step 2 derives the scattering matrix elements using transmission line theory and power conservation principles. Step 3 demonstrates the substitution of the operating frequency into the S-parameter expressions, showing the intermediate complex values before conversion. This two-stage presentation formatˇŞfirst showing the complex S-parameter values, then demonstrating the logarithmic conversion to decibel formˇŞhelps users understand the complete transformation from theoretical expressions to practical measurement units. Step 4 extends the analysis across the frequency range, with results displayed in the S-parameter plot positioned directly below the input controls.
The frequency-dependent S-parameter plot uses a custom painter to render multiple curves simultaneously. When S?? and S?? have identical values due to circuit symmetry, the overlapping curves appear as a single line. The graphical visualization component (rd_painter.dart) renders an animated representation of the three-port network. 5.5.2 Wilkinson Divider Module

The Wilkinson divider module implements a three-port network with improved isolation compared to the resistive divider, achieved through quarter-wave transmission line sections and an isolation resistor. The module structure mirrors the resistive divider's organization but incorporates more complex S-parameter expressions.
The input interface (wd_input.dart) provides the same frequency and impedance controls as the resistive divider, maintaining consistency across modules. The port selection mechanism allows users to observe how the circuit responds when different ports are excited.
The calculation steps (wd_steps.dart) follow the same four-step structure established in the resistive divider module. However, the S-parameter expressions in Step 2 are more complex, involving frequency-dependent phase terms due to the quarter-wave transformer sections. Step 3 shows the numerical evaluation at the selected frequency, explicitly displaying both the complex S-parameter values and their conversion to decibel form using the 20ˇÁlog?? transformation.
A significant improvement in this module's implementation was relocating the S-parameter plot from within Step 4 to a position directly below the input controls. This change, implemented in wd_main.dart, ensures visual consistency with the other three modules. The plot component (wd_plot.dart) uses the same rendering approach as the resistive divider, with frequency on the horizontal axis and S-parameter magnitude in dB on the vertical axis. The same note about overlapping curves appears adjacent to the plot.
The animated visualization (wd_painter.dart) renders the three-port network with quarter-wave transmission line sections explicitly shown. The wave animation accounts for the phase shifts introduced by these transmission line sections, providing a more accurate representation of the signal propagation compared to the resistive divider's simpler topology.

5.5.3 Rat-Race Coupler Module

The rat-race coupler module implements a four-port network with a ring topology, providing power division with specific phase relationships between output ports. This module required particular attention to the user interface design due to the increased number of ports.
The input interface (coupler_input.dart) initially presented all four ports in a simple list format. Based on instructor feedback, the interface was redesigned to use a more intuitive button-based selection mechanism. Four clearly labeled buttonsˇŞ"Port 1", "Port 2", "Port 3", and "Port 4"ˇŞallow users to select the excited port, with the active selection highlighted in red. This visual feedback immediately clarifies which port is receiving the input signal.
The calculation steps (coupler_step.dart) maintain the four-step structure but accommodate the increased complexity of a four-port network. The S-parameter matrix is now 4ˇÁ4, requiring sixteen individual elements. Step 3 shows the numerical evaluation for all sixteen parameters, with each conversion from complex magnitude to decibel form explicitly displayed. The note about overlapping curves is particularly relevant here, as the circuit's symmetry often results in multiple S-parameters having identical values.
The S-parameter plot appears below the input controls, consistent with the other modules. The plot displays all relevant S-parameters across the frequency range, with different colors distinguishing the various curves.
The visualization component (coupler_source.dart) renders the ring topology with four ports positioned at 90-degree intervals. During development, a bug was discovered where output waves would disappear at frequencies around 30 GHz. Investigation revealed that the wave amplitude calculation was producing values below the rendering threshold. The fix involved adjusting the amplitude scaling and adding a minimum threshold:
final y = 10.0 * max(mag, 0.1) * sin((t / wavelength) * 2 * pi + phase);
This ensures that even small-magnitude signals remain visible, though the high-frequency wave visibility note still applies when the wavelength becomes extremely short.

5.5.4 Branch-Line Hybrid Module

The branch-line hybrid module implements a four-port network with quadrature (90-degree) phase difference between output ports, commonly used in balanced amplifiers and mixers. The implementation follows the same architectural pattern as the rat-race coupler but with different S-parameter expressions reflecting the branch-line topology.
The input interface (bl_input.dart) uses the same four-button port selection design as the rat-race coupler, providing consistent user experience across the four-port modules. The frequency and impedance controls maintain the same ranges and behavior as the other modules.
The calculation steps (bl_steps.dart) present the branch-line hybrid's S-parameter derivation in four stages. The S-parameter expressions differ from the rat-race coupler due to the different transmission line lengths and connection topology, but the presentation format remains consistent. Step 3 shows the complete two-stage calculation: first the complex S-parameter values at the selected frequency, then the conversion to decibel form with the 20ˇÁlog?? operation explicitly shown for each parameter.
The S-parameter plot (bl_plot.dart) appears below the input controls and includes the note about overlapping curves. The plot rendering uses the same custom painter approach as the other modules, ensuring visual consistency throughout the application.
The visualization component (bl_painter.dart) renders the branch-line topology with four ports and the characteristic quarter-wave branch sections. The wave animation accounts for the 90-degree phase relationships between ports, providing visual confirmation of the quadrature operation. The high-frequency wave visibility note appears in the same location as in the other modules.
All four modules share common design patterns: consistent input interfaces, four-step calculation presentations with explicit dB conversion steps, S-parameter plots positioned below input controls, and animated visualizations with appropriate notes about visual limitations. This modular consistency facilitates user learning and reduces cognitive load when switching between different circuit types.

5.6 Validation Approach

The validation approach adopted in this study was based on analytical consistency rather than on hardware fabrication or full-wave electromagnetic simulation. This approach is appropriate because the developed application is intended primarily as a theory-driven educational tool, and its outputs are derived from ideal microwave network relationships.
In practical terms, validation was performed by comparing the implemented software outputs with expected theoretical behavior under selected representative conditions. Particular emphasis was placed on the modules with meaningful variable inputs, since these provide the clearest basis for checking whether the implemented analytical logic responds correctly to user-defined conditions.
For this reason, detailed case-based validation was focused mainly on the Wilkinson Divider and Rat-Race Coupler modules. The Resistive Divider and Branch-Line Hybrid modules were reviewed primarily in terms of logical consistency and functional realization, since their current implementations are closer to fixed or standard analytical demonstrations than to generalized parametric design tools.
This form of validation should therefore be understood as software-level verification within the scope of ideal analytical microwave models. It does not represent fabricated hardware testing, nor does it attempt to replace professional full-wave simulation. Nevertheless, it is appropriate and sufficient for the educational and analytical

objectives of the present project.

5.9 Discussion

The developed application demonstrates that microwave passive component theory can be translated into a practical and interactive analytical tool using a lightweight cross-platform software framework. One of the main contributions of the system lies in its integration of multiple representative microwave components within a single environment. This allows users to compare different forms of power division and coupling behavior without relying solely on static textbook equations or multiple separate tools.
A particular strength of the application is its educational orientation. Because the software is based on direct analytical relationships, users can observe the connection between selected input conditions and resulting microwave behavior immediately. This is especially evident in the Wilkinson Divider and Rat-Race Coupler modules, where changes in split ratio or excitation state produce clear and interpretable differences in the displayed outputs. Such interactivity improves conceptual understanding and supports the pedagogical motivation of the project.

At the same time, the current implementation has several limitations. The analytical models are based on idealized circuit theory and therefore do not include conductor loss, dielectric loss, fabrication tolerance, discontinuity effects, bandwidth limitations, or full electromagnetic field behavior. For this reason, the application should not be interpreted as a replacement for professional simulation environments such as ADS, CST, or HFSS. Instead, it should be regarded as an educational and preliminary analytical platform.
Another limitation is that the present validation is based on selected theoretical software-level checks rather than on fabricated hardware measurement or extensive external benchmarking. This is consistent with the scope of the project, but it also means that the current evaluation is focused on correctness within ideal analytical conditions rather than real-world implementation performance. Future work could therefore extend the application by incorporating broader validation, comparison with external simulation tools, or user-based evaluation.
Despite these limitations, the developed system successfully achieves its main objective. It provides an accessible and structured environment for studying the behavior of representative microwave passive components and demonstrates the potential of Flutter-based engineering software in educational contexts.
